1. Definitions
 Robot: An automated machine designed to execute specific tasks with speed, precision, and little to no human
intervention.
 Robotics: The technical field that deals directly with the design, engineering, and operation of robots.
2. Applications and Uses of Robots in Society
Robotics technology significantly impacts various industries by automating tasks, reducing labor costs, and boosting
efficiency. Notable applications include:
 Manufacturing: Used to automate repetitive tasks on assembly lines, such as welding and painting, to increase
productivity and quality.
 Transportation: Implemented in self-driving cars, drones, and autonomous vehicles to reduce traffic congestion
and improve overall safety.
 Healthcare: Assists medical professionals with surgical procedures, patient rehabilitation, monitoring vital signs,
and administering medication.
 Agriculture: Deployed to handle crop planting, harvesting, growth monitoring, and lowering labor expenses.
 Construction: Automates physically demanding, labor-intensive tasks like bricklaying and concrete pouring.
 Space Exploration: Used to safely explore hostile environments such as other planets, moons, and asteroids.
 Service Industry: Utilized in banks, hotels, and malls to guide customers, provide information, and answer general
queries.
 Military and Defense: Deployed for high-risk operations including surveillance, reconnaissance, and bomb
disposal.
3. Prospects and Challenges of Using Robots
While robots provide great opportunities, deploying them (especially in educational environments) brings distinct
challenges:
 Training Demands: Tutors require proper training to use robots as teaching aids, and untrained learners may
struggle to interact with the technology effectively.
 Ongoing Costs & Maintenance: Industrial setups face continuing maintenance, operational, and cybersecurity
protection expenses. Similarly, high maintenance costs limit the widespread deployment of educational robots.
 Technology Reliability: As mechanical devices, robots are prone to failures at any time, making their long-term
integrity a constant challenge.
 The "Uncanny Valley" Effect: Educational robots built with human-like appearances can evoke fear in students,
which can actively impede the learning process.
 Safety Risks: Physical hazards, such as robots built with sharp edges, can cause severe harm or lacerations to
young children, discouraging them from using the technology.
